1. The Buddha Knew Vastly More Than He Taught

He compares a few leaves in his hand to the number of leaves in the siMsapa grove above him in kosambI as a metaphor comparing what he has actually known through direct experience, compared to the subset of which he actually taught. (SN 56.31 sIsapAvanasutta)

2. Resolving Uncertainty

In a case of uncertainty, he Buddha tells the kAlAmas not to accept claims by transitive methods, but instead through direct experience by way of practice. (AN 3:66 kAlAmasutta)

3. Avoid Extreme Conclusions

Such as, “only this is true; anything else is worthless.” For there are things which are true yet are not held in favor, as well as things untrue yet held favorably. One who seeks the truth avoids this type of position. If through transitive methods one has gained conviction in a view, it is only proper for him to proclaim that conviction as it is, without claiming it to be the truth. (MN 95 caMkIsutta)

4. Rejection of the Epistemic Authority of the Brahmin Hyms

The Buddha rejects the claim of the Brahmins that the Brahmin hyms* are infallible and that all else is worthless. He compares those Brahmins and their oral tradition to a row of blind men, none of which can see. (MN 95 caMkIsutta)

6. The Happiness of the Devas

Even when one passes away from there, he enjoys an incredible birth here in the human realm, set up ideally for going back up again.

Then the Buddha, picking up a stone the size of his palm, addressed the mendicants, “What do you think, mendicants? Which is bigger: the stone the size of my palm that I’ve picked up, or the Himalayas, the king of mountains?”

“Sir, the stone you’ve picked up is tiny. Compared to the Himalayas, it doesn’t count, it’s not worth a fraction, there’s no comparison.”

“In the same way, compared to the happiness of heaven, the pleasure and happiness experienced by a wheel-turning monarch due to those seven treasures and those four blessings doesn’t even count, it’s not even a fraction, there’s no comparison.

And suppose that astute person, after a very long time, returned to the human realm. They’d be reborn in a well-to-do family of aristocrats, brahmins, or householders—rich, affluent, and wealthy, with lots of gold and silver, lots of property and assets, and lots of money and grain. And they’d be attractive, good-looking, lovely, of surpassing beauty. They’d get to have food, drink, clothes, and vehicles; garlands, perfumes, and makeup; and a bed, house, and lighting. And they do good things by way of body, speech, and mind. When their body breaks up, after death, they’re reborn in a good place, a heavenly realm.

Suppose a gambler was to win a big pile of money with the first perfect hand. Such a perfect hand is trivial compared to the perfect hand whereby an astute person, when their body breaks up, after death, is reborn in a good place, a heavenly realm. This is the total fulfillment of the astute person’s level.” (MN 129 bAlapaNDitasutta)

7. Words of Others

8. Discord Guy

From this morning’s visit with Arya Santideva, via his Bodhisattvacaryavatara:

So when will I bring peace to those Ravaged by fires of suffering By showering down from clouds of merit A rain of comforts to sustain them?

May everyone in all directions Who’s anguished or diseased In body or mind gain, through my merit, An ocean of happiness.

For as long as space endures, As long as there are beings, I will remain to eliminate All their myriad sufferings.

Thoughts: Spiritual growth endures and blossoms only when it is cultivated selflessly and altruistically for the sake of others. When viewed as an isolated acquisition meant for me and me alone, it withers and weakens. But when it is harnessed for the betterment and uplifting of all beings – when it is allowed to spread its wings in service to others – it grows and shines forth with an immeasurable potential.

8.1. dharmakIrti

Accepting the authority of the Vedas, believing in individual agency, hoping for merit from bathing, taking pride in caste, undertaking rites for the removal of evils: these are the five signs of stupidity, the destruction of intelligence. (Wikipedia)

8.2. Bhavaviveka

Later Buddhist authors like Bhavaviveka and Saṅghabhadra argued that the Vedas were authored by ancient people who were spiritually and philosophically unqualified as well as being morally deficient.[76] In his critique, Bhavaviveka actually draws on some passages found in the Hindu Samkhyakarika and in the work of Gaudapada, which holds that Vedic sacrifice is impure and of “mixed nature” (Sanskrit: miśrībhāva). Bhavaviveka points to the presence of killing (found in various sacrifices), sexual promiscuity (in a Vedic ritual called Gosava) and use of alcohol (in a rite called Sautrāmaṇī) in the Vedas to argue that they were authored by evil people and compares them to the foreign scriptures of the "Magas" (i.e. the Persian Magi).[77] Furthermore, in the ninth chapter of his Madhyamakahrdayakārikā (Verses on the Heart of the Middle Way), Bhavaviveka critiques the idea that bad karma can be removed through Vedic ritual, through devotion (bhakti) to the gods or by meditating on the gods and their mantras.[78] (Wikipedia)

9. Misc

9.1. Intentional Falsehood in the Suttas

There may be some false content intentionally inserted into the Suttas for the better. The Suttas don’t seem to strive for exact accuracy. Rather, they serve to liberate beings from saMsAra, and if a few white lies here and there would make it more effective, then perhaps they might have included it in.

9.2. Superior to Sacrifice

Revering those of perfected minds is far, far more fruitful than even a hunred years of sacrifice and oblation. (Dhp 106-108)
